His army retreated without a fight. His nobles are questioning his judgment. He needs to show that he’s still in control.” She shrugged. “It’s politics. The kind you’re going to have to get used to.”

“I’m not a politician.”

“No. You’re something better.” She smiled, thin and sharp. “You’re the one they can’t predict. Use that.”

She stood, brushing crumbs from her tunic. “I’ll meet you at the council. Don’t be late. Lord Malachar is patient, but even he has limits.”

She walked away, and Kaelen watched her go, something unsettled in his chest.

“She’s interested in you,” Rin said, her voice casual.

“She’s interested in what I represent.”

“Same thing.” Rin stole a piece of cheese from his plate. “She’s been watching you since the beginning. Waiting to see what you’d become. Now she’s seen.” She met his eyes. “The question is: does she want to be part of it, or does she want to use it?”

Seraphina’s hand found his under the table. “We’ll watch her. Together.”

Kaelen nodded, but his mind was already turning. Vex’ahlia had given him the key. She had stood with him when the army came. But she was still Church, still bound by oaths he didn’t fully understand. Trust was a luxury he couldn’t afford—not completely.

But he was learning that he didn’t have to carry everything alone.

He looked at Seraphina, at Rin, at Elara. They were watching him, waiting, their faces open in a way they hadn’t been before. The masks were down, for all of them.

“Let’s go see what the demon lords want,” he said, standing. “And then let’s see about building something that lasts.”
